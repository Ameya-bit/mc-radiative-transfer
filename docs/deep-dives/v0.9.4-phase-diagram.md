## 1. What the map asks

The two anchors are single points in a much larger space. The project's headline is that the
discriminator between "systematic lands in PF" and "systematic hides in shape" is **not**
whether a single spot eclipses, but whether the two spots **tile** the rotation — when one
dips behind the star, is the other bright enough that the combined flux never reaches zero?
v0.9.4 makes that claim general by sweeping the geometry plane and coloring each cell by where
the systematic lands.

**The canonical model.** Two equal-weight point spots at the **same** colatitude θ, separated
in azimuth by **Δφ**, at a fixed spacetime (inclination i, compactness u). For each cell we
build the single-spot isotropic and realistic (limb-darkened, τ = 10) base profiles once, then
each Δφ column is the cheap additive sum of the base with its phase-rolled copy. The color is

    ΔPF(Δφ, θ) = PF_real − PF_iso

evaluated at the profile-shape optical depth τ = `SHAPE_TAU` = 10.

## 2. The analytic boundary — the tiling criterion as a curve

Two identical anti-phased spots each spend a fraction `f_ecl(θ)` of the cycle eclipsed behind
the star, their dark windows centered half a turn apart. The combined flux touches zero —
pinning PF = 1 for *both* beamings, so ΔPF collapses to ≈ 0 — exactly when those two dark
windows overlap, i.e. when the separation is smaller than a single spot's own eclipse fraction:

    Δφ < f_ecl(θ)   →  saturated:  systematic hides in waveform shape
    Δφ > f_ecl(θ)   →  tiling:     systematic stays live in the pulsed fraction

So the saturation boundary is simply

    Δφ_crit(θ) = f_ecl(θ),

the single-spot eclipse fraction already computed by
`anchor_lib.single_spot_eclipsed_fraction`. The figure overlays this analytic curve (solid
cyan) on the **independent** numerical contour where the two-spot isotropic flux floor
F_min/F_max → 0 (white dashed). The two agree across both panels — an internal check that the
analytic criterion really is what the simulated flux does.

## 3. What the map shows

Two panels share axes and color scale, one per representative spacetime so each real star sits
on a map drawn at its own kind of spacetime:

- **J0740-like** (compact, near edge-on: u = 0.49, i = 87.6°). The eclipse fraction is tiny
  (bending keeps spots visible almost all rotation), so the boundary hugs the left edge and
  nearly the whole plane is the tiling regime — bright ΔPF. **Both J0740 fits (Δφ ≈ 0.44) land
  deep in the bright region: ΔPF = +0.16 (Riley), +0.23 (Miller).**
- **J0030-like** (moderate, intermediate i: u = 0.31, i = 53.9°). The boundary sweeps up and to
  the right: at the far-hemisphere colatitudes where J0030's spots sit (θ ≈ 130°–167°), the
  eclipse fraction is large, so the saturated (black, ΔPF ≈ 0) region reaches past Δφ ≈ 0.45.
  **Both J0030 fits fall on the saturated side: ΔPF = +0.00.**

Each star marker's ΔPF is computed from that fit's **full** geometry (its own spots, i, u),
not the canonical equal-θ cell, so the markers carry the true result — they reproduce the
v0.9.1 and v0.9.2 anchor numbers exactly. The plotting colatitude is the mean of the two spots;
the separation is folded into [0, 0.5] (a pair at Δφ and at 1−Δφ are mirror images with
identical PF), which is why Miller J0740's Δφ = 0.558 plots at 0.44.

## 4. Scope — which field configurations the background slice covers

The colatitude × separation plane is the natural space for all three canonical hot-spot
arrangements, but the *background heatmap* fixes both spots at the SAME colatitude θ, so it is
a slice:

- A **centered dipole** has antipodal poles — colatitudes θ and 180°−θ, separation 0.5 — so it
  is *not* a single point on the equal-θ map (it coincides only at the equatorial corner
  θ = 90°, Δφ = 0.5). The antipodal/dipole case is the validation limit (reproduce Poutanen &
  Beloborodov 2006), handled in v0.9.5 — not the headline.
- **Offset-dipole and multipolar same-hemisphere pairs** (J0030-like) live directly on this
  slice — the regime where the systematic actually varies, and the novel result.

The underlying machinery (`anchor_lib.multi_spot_flux`) has no such restriction — it takes each
spot's own colatitude (e.g. J0740 Riley's 77°/108°), so the real-star markers carry the true
unequal-colatitude ΔPF even where the equal-θ background is only schematic.
