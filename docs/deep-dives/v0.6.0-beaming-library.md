# Deep Dive — v0.6.0: The Beaming-Function Library and a Thin-τ Anomaly

> Companion to the [v0.6.0 progress-log entry](../../README.md#v060--the-beaming-function-as-a-function-of-optical-depth).
> We sweep the optical depth τ to build a library `I(μ; τ)` for pulse-profile synthesis, find
> that the thick end reproduces theory, and uncover an unphysical **limb-brightening** at small
> τ that traces back to the injection law. Code: `scripts/tau_sweep.py`,
> `src/mcrt/beaming.py`, `src/mcrt/monte_carlo.py`.
>
> **Builds on:** [v0.5.1: Beaming Function, Flux vs. Intensity](v0.5.1-beaming-correction.md)
> (the validated single-τ extraction this generalises).

---

## 1. Why sweep τ at all

The validated beaming function `I(μ)` is for one atmosphere thickness. Pulse-profile synthesis
needs `I(μ)` as a function of the scattering optical depth **τ**, for two reasons:

1. **The shape of `I(μ)` is *set* by τ.** Limb darkening is produced by scattering. The more a
   photon scatters before escaping, the more grazing escapes are suppressed and the steeper the
   limb darkening. So thin and thick atmospheres beam differently, and a single curve cannot
   describe both.
2. **We do not know the target star's τ in advance**, and the size of the
   realistic-vs-isotropic effect (the project's actual result) itself depends on τ. We therefore
   tabulate `I(μ)` across τ once, as a **library**, so downstream pulse-profile code can look up
   (interpolate) the curve for any chosen τ instead of re-running the Monte Carlo every time.

`scripts/tau_sweep.py` sweeps `τ ∈ {0.1, 0.3, 1, 3, 10, 30}` at 200k photons each (fixed seed),
reuses `extract_intensity` / `fit_limb_darkening_slope` from `mcrt.beaming`, and saves the
family of curves to `data/beaming_library.npz` (arrays `tau_values`, `mu_centers`,
`intensity_by_tau`, `b_of_tau`).

## 2. What the sweep shows

![Beaming-function library: I(μ) vs. optical depth τ](../../data/beaming_tau_curves.png)

![Limb-darkening slope b vs. optical depth τ](../../data/beaming_slope_vs_tau.png)

The fitted limb-darkening slope `b` (in `I(μ) = a(1 + bμ)`):

| τ | 0.1 | 0.3 | 1.0 | 3.0 | 10.0 | 30.0 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| `b` | −0.88 | −0.53 | +0.53 | +1.66 | +1.69 | +1.75 |

**The thick end is correct.** For τ ≥ 3 the curves collapse onto the Chandrasekhar H-function
(RMS deviation 0.03–0.08), and `b → ~1.75`, just above the Eddington value of 1.5 as expected
(the H-function is slightly steeper than the linear law). The τ = 10 point reproduces the
validated v0.5.1 result. This is the physically relevant regime for a real neutron-star
atmosphere, and it behaves.

**The thin end does not.** At τ = 0.1 and 0.3 the slope is *negative* — `b < 0` means the fitted
intensity *rises* toward the limb (μ → 0) instead of falling. That is **limb brightening**: the
model claims the star is brighter at its edge than face-on. For a weakly-scattering atmosphere
fed by a thermal source that is meant to be isotropic, this is unphysical.

> **`b` is a slope, not a brightness.** Every `I(μ)` value in the library is positive. A negative
> `b` is the *tilt* of the curve (brighter toward the limb), not a negative intensity.

## 3. Diagnosis: the thin limit measures the *source*, not the atmosphere

At small τ almost nothing scatters. A diagnostic run at τ = 0.1 shows **84% of escaping photons
never scattered at all**. So the thin-τ emergent field is essentially the **injected source**,
passed straight through — the sweep is testing our boundary condition, not transport physics.

That turns attention to how photons are injected. The engine (`monte_carlo.py`, the loop body)
launches photons with

```python
costheta = np.random.uniform(0, 1)   # μ uniform on [0, 1]
```

i.e. an **equal number of photons per unit μ**. v0.2.0 described this as "isotropic over the
upward hemisphere," and it *is* isotropic in one sense — uniform per unit **solid angle**
(`dΩ = dμ dφ`). But that is **not** the same as an **isotropic specific intensity** source, and
the difference is the whole bug.

**The key fact.** A surface that is equally bright in every direction (isotropic *intensity*)
does **not** emit an equal *number* of photons in every direction. The photon count crossing the
surface into a direction is the brightness times a projection (foreshortening) factor:

$$ N(\mu)\,d\mu \;\propto\; I(\mu)\,\mu\,d\mu. $$

So an isotropic-intensity source (`I = const`) emits photons with `N(μ) ∝ μ` — *more* straight
out (μ → 1), *fewer* grazing (μ → 0). Our uniform-in-μ injection instead over-produces grazing
photons. When the thin atmosphere passes that source through and `extract_intensity` divides the
binned counts by μ (the v0.5.1 flux→intensity step), uniform counts become `I ∝ 1/μ`, which
diverges toward the limb — the limb brightening we see.

The interaction is clean enough to isolate. Sampling the source angle two ways and running each
through the same flux→intensity extraction (the τ → 0 limit):

| injection law | raw counts (low μ → high μ) | fitted `b` |
|---|---|---|
| `μ ~ uniform(0, 1)` — **current engine** | flat (`~24800 … ~25000`) | **−1.02** (limb-brightened) |
| `μ ~ sqrt(U)`, i.e. `N(μ) ∝ μ` — isotropic intensity | ramps `~1200 … ~49000` | **−0.00** (flat = isotropic) |

The `sqrt(U)` source recovers a flat `I(μ)` — exactly the isotropic baseline the thin limit
*should* produce.

**Why the thick end was never affected.** At τ = 10–30 a photon scatters dozens of times and
completely forgets its injection direction; the emergent field is fixed by transfer physics
(Chandrasekhar/Eddington) regardless of the source. The injection law only leaks through where
scattering is too weak to erase it — i.e. thin τ. So the validated v0.5.1 result stands.

## 4. Status and the planned fix

- **Library built and saved** (`data/beaming_library.npz`), reproducible from a fixed seed.
- **Thick-τ behaviour validated** against Chandrasekhar H; τ = 10 matches v0.5.1.
- **Known defect (this version):** thin-τ limb brightening, traced to a uniform-in-μ injection
  that represents isotropic *solid angle* rather than isotropic *intensity*. Documented here as
  a baseline before any behavioural change.

**Next (v0.6.x):** change the injection to an isotropic-intensity source, `costheta = sqrt(U)`,
regenerate the library, and confirm `b(τ)` rises cleanly from ~0 (thin/isotropic) to ~1.75
(thick). If more than one source distribution is worth comparing, each lands as its own `0.6.x`
entry; the working fix becomes **v0.6.1**. Once the library is physically clean across all τ, it
feeds pulse-profile synthesis.
