## 1. What this anchor asks

v0.9.0 showed the beaming systematic is real and, at intermediate geometries, large
(ΔPF up to +0.16). But those geometries were chosen for convenience — always-visible,
spanning weak-to-strong modulation. The honest follow-up is to plant the swap at a
geometry someone actually measured. PSR J0030+0451 is *the* canonical NICER
mass–radius pulsar, fit independently by two teams:

- **Riley et al. (2019, ApJL 887 L21)** — X-PSI / Amsterdam, ST+PST model.
- **Miller et al. (2019, ApJL 887 L24)** — Illinois–Maryland, three-oval-spot model.

We take each fit's spacetime (compactness `u = 2GM/Rc²`), observer inclination `i`,
and hot-spot colatitudes from its published tables, and run the isotropic→realistic
swap there. This is **differential, not a fit** (`to_finish.md §6`): a relative change
at a realistic operating point, not an inversion of J0030's data.

| | M (M⊙) | R (km) | u | i | spot colatitudes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Riley 2019 (ST+PST, Table 2) | 1.34 | 12.71 | 0.312 | 53.9° | 127.8°, 166.7° |
| Miller 2019 (3-spot, Table 8) | 1.44 | 13.02 | 0.326 | 50.3° | 130.0°, 138.5° |

## 2. The surprise: J0030's spots dive behind the star

Both teams — independently — place J0030's hot spots in the *same* hemisphere, the
one tilted **away** from us (colatitude well past 90°), viewed nearly edge-on
(i ≈ 50–54°). As the star spins, such a spot swings to ψ ≈ 180° — directly behind the
star. Light bending at u ≈ 0.31 lets us see only ~27° around the back, nowhere near
enough, so the spot is **eclipsed for ~45% of every rotation** (`single_spot_eclipsed_fraction`
returns 0.45 for Riley, 0.46 for Miller).

A spot that sets drives the flux to **zero**, and a zero minimum forces

```
PF = (F_max − 0) / (F_max + 0) = 1
```

for *both* the isotropic and the realistic run. So **ΔPF = 0**: the pulsed-fraction
systematic vanishes — not because beaming stopped mattering, but because PF has hit
its ceiling and cannot register the difference.

**Two spots do not rescue it.** One might hope the second published spot fills the
first's eclipse. It does not: both spots hug the *same* pole (Riley 127.8°/166.7°,
Miller 130.0°/138.5°), so even ~0.45 cycle apart in azimuth their visible windows
leave a gap where the whole star is dark. The summed two-spot flux still touches zero;
PF stays pinned at 1 (`test_j0030_two_spots_still_saturate`).

---

## 3. This is the Rung C physics, not a new failure

v0.9.0 already saw this coming. Its three-geometry table showed ΔPF is **largest at
the mid geometry and smallest at the high-contrast one** — only +0.018 — precisely
because the high-contrast spot already sat at `PF_iso ≈ 0.97`, with no headroom left.
**J0030 is the extreme of that same corner:** so deeply eclipsing that PF is pinned at
exactly 1. The pulsed-fraction systematic is small at J0030 for the *same reason* it
was small for the high-contrast Rung C case — saturation, not absence.

So the systematic's **observability is geometry-dependent**: it lands in the pulsed
fraction for intermediate geometries and is invisible there for eclipsing ones.

---

## 4. Where the systematic actually lives at J0030: the waveform shape

The beaming difference does not disappear when PF saturates — it moves into the
*shape* of the visible pulse. Measuring the change between the two peak-normalized
waveforms (`waveform_shape_change`, eclipse-immune by construction):

| | shape change (RMS) | shape change (max-local) | at τ |
|---|---|---|---|
| Riley 2019 | 0.065 | 0.136 | 10 |
| Miller 2019 | 0.075 | 0.181 | 30 |

Assuming isotropy distorts J0030's visible waveform by **~6–8% RMS (up to ~14–18%
locally)** — and, like the v0.9.0 ΔPF, it grows with optical depth as the
limb-darkening slope `b(τ)` steepens. That is the real, knob-free Rung D number: the
scattering-beaming systematic on a J0030-like geometry is a *waveform-shape* effect of
several percent, not a pulsed-fraction effect.

## 5. Caveat: a background would expose a sign-flipped PF systematic

Real spots are extended and sit on an unpulsed background (instrument + non-eclipsing
emission), so J0030's true minimum is non-zero rather than a hard eclipse. We do **not**
model a background — its level is unconstrained, and choosing one would be a tuned knob
that crosses into fitting. But it is worth showing what it would do (panel C): adding a
common background `B` and watching ΔPF(B). At B = 0 the eclipse pins ΔPF = 0; as `B`
lifts the floor and un-saturates the pulse, a PF systematic re-emerges:

| background (fraction of peak) | Riley ΔPF | Miller ΔPF |
|---|---|---|
| 5% | −0.036 | −0.061 |
| 30% | −0.091 | −0.143 |

Two things to note. First, the magnitude is real (−0.06 to −0.14) but **depends on the
assumed background** — hence a caveat, not a headline. Second, the sign is **negative —
opposite to v0.9.0's +0.16**. That sign flip is physical: J0030's eclipsing spots only
ever reach modest emission angles (μ ≲ 0.45), so limb darkening makes their *peak*
dimmer rather than sharpening a high-μ-to-low-μ contrast. Same physics, opposite
consequence — one more illustration that the systematic is geometry-dependent in
*sign* as well as size.

## 6. Honest scope (carried from `to_finish.md §7, §10`)

- **Point spots, not the published shapes.** We reduce each fit's spots to points at
  their center colatitudes. The real spots are an extended disk + crescent (Riley) and
  ovals (Miller); the point reduction over-sharpens the eclipse (real edges peek over
  the horizon). Stated as a limitation; it does not change the qualitative result that
  J0030 is eclipse-saturated.
- **Differential, not a fit.** We adopt published geometry and swap the beaming; we do
  not invert J0030's data or tune M–R.
- **Monochromatic / bolometric, conservative Thomson, slow-rotation Schwarzschild** —
  the same idealizations as v0.9.0 (energy-integrated, ϖ = 1, no Doppler/oblateness).

---

## 7. Status

- **Result delivered:** at J0030's published geometry (both Riley and Miller) the
  point-spot pulse is eclipse-saturated (PF = 1, ΔPF ≈ 0 in pulsed fraction); the
  beaming systematic instead reshapes the visible waveform by ~6–8% RMS, growing with
  τ. A background would expose a sign-flipped PF systematic of −0.06 to −0.14 (caveat).
- **The narrative:** the systematic's observability is geometry-dependent — PF-visible
  at intermediate geometries (v0.9.0), shape-visible at J0030.
- **Tests: 43/43 pass** (four new: single-spot eclipse, two-spot saturation, shape
  changes while PF does not, and the azimuth-roll mechanism).
- **No core change** — two-spot synthesis is script-level (`compute_profile` + `np.roll`).

**Next:** v1.0.0 — the paper. Frame v0.9.0 (the systematic, where it is PF-visible) and
v0.9.1 (its geometry-dependence and where it hides) as the two halves of one result.
