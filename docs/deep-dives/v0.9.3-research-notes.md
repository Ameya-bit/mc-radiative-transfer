## 1. Direction — the NICER comparison is differential, not a verification (and always was)

A natural worry is that the project drifted: the original task list
([`../monte_carlo_nicer.pdf`](../monte_carlo_nicer.pdf)) has a section titled "Comparison with
NICER Observations," yet what we report is an isotropic-vs-realistic *difference*, not a match
to a NICER measurement. Reading the task list settles it — there was no drift:

- The stated **Objective** is "to quantify how radiative transfer-induced beaming alters pulse
  profiles and pulsed fractions," and **§4 ("Comparison Models")** is literally "repeat
  pulse-profile calculations assuming isotropic emission … quantify differences in pulse shape
  and pulsed fraction." That *is* the experiment we run.
- **§5 ("Comparison with NICER Observations")** names PSR J0030+0451 as the target and says to
  "**emphasize relative changes rather than full parameter fitting**," with the goal of showing
  the systematic reaches "levels comparable to NICER uncertainties." Differential, from the start.

Why a literal "match our PF to NICER's PF" is not well-posed — the deeper reason §5 was right:

1. **Forward vs. inverse.** Our model is the *forward* map (geometry → light curve → PF); NICER
   inference is the *inverse* (light curve → mass, radius, geometry). To produce a PF to
   compare you must first adopt a geometry, which only exists as an *output* of the inverse
   problem — itself fit under some assumed beaming. The comparison is therefore circular and
   conditional on borrowing the answer.
2. **Non-uniqueness.** There is no single "NICER geometry" for a star: independent teams
   disagree (Riley vs. Miller), single fits are multi-modal (J0740's bimodal phases, ~4-fold
   degeneracy), and the spot *shapes* differ by model (J0030 ST+PST vs. three-oval). Nothing to
   verify against.

The resolution — and the project's standing scope decision (`to_finish.md` §6, "differential,
not a fit") — is to hold a *published* geometry fixed, swap **only** the beaming, and report
ΔPF. That isolates the beaming systematic without solving (or trusting one solution of) the
inverse problem, exactly as §5 prescribed. Full M–R inference / fitting real data is explicitly
out of scope (`to_finish.md` §7); inverting NICER is noted only as possible *future* work
([`../proposal/future_directions_after_completion.md`](../proposal/future_directions_after_completion.md)).

What genuinely changed: the early `RNAA_draft.pdf`'s fabricated "~15%" number and its
magnetic-anisotropy (Ho & Lai) framing were retired (`to_finish.md` §10) — and anisotropy was
only ever an *optional* task in the list (`monte_carlo_nicer.pdf` §2), never a goal.

## 2. Spot-colatitude convention

Colatitude is the polar angle from the **spin pole**, **0–180°** (90° = equator) — not 0–90°
(that is latitude; colatitude = 90° − latitude). Riley and Miller both use it, and Miller's
spot-centre prior of **0.1–3.1 rad** makes the full range explicit. The observer **inclination**
is the same kind of angle (the observer's colatitude). The full table — including the angles
that are *not* colatitudes (ψ, α/μ, azimuth) and the ζ "radius vs. viewing-angle" collision — is
in [`../references.md`](../references.md#angle--coordinate-conventions). This 0–180° convention
is what lets a single number place a spot in the far hemisphere (θ > 90°), the crux of §3.

## 3. J0030's geometry — non-dipolar, multipolar-preferred, origin unknown

Both J0030 fits place **both** hot regions in the *same* rotational hemisphere (colatitudes
128°/167° Riley, 130°/138° Miller) — non-antipodal. A centered dipole forces antipodal poles
(colatitudes θ and 180°−θ), so it cannot produce this: Riley's 127.8° spot would need a partner
near 52°, not the observed 167°.

- **Riley 2019** states antipodal models are "strongly disfavored," implying "a complex offset
  dipolar and multipolar field structure."
- **Miller 2019** independently infers "at least an offset dipole, and may have higher moments."
- **Bilous 2019** (companion paper, now in `references.md`) shows the inferred heating cannot be
  produced by magnetospheric currents at open-field-line footpoints for *any* canonical global
  field (centered dipole, static, or vacuum-retarded), so the data prefer an offset/multipolar
  field — but it does **not** determine the actual field or its origin, and flags that the
  result is conditional on R19's atmosphere/shape/background assumptions.

Bottom line: J0030's geometry is confirmed non-dipolar but otherwise unexplained. This is *not*
the project's problem to solve — we adopt the published geometry as a fixed input (§1), and the
beaming-swap result is differential, holding regardless of *why* the spots sit together. The
geometry's shape/assumption uncertainties are inherited, and bounded by the planned finite-cap
robustness check.

## Where this leaves the paper

The three notes converge on the headline: realistic beaming produces a measurable
pulsed-fraction systematic (ΔPF ≈ +0.16), and whether it is observable is set by a geometric
rule — whether the two spots **tile** the rotation. It lands in the pulsed fraction when they
tile (J0740) and hides in the waveform shape when they do not (J0030's same-hemisphere spots).
The geometry phase diagram that turns the two anchors into that general tiling / PF-saturation
rule is the next build.
